Your own design bible already points this way: the ratified aesthetic memory is "Guix TUI + ksbc palette — crimson/gold on near-black" (DESIGN_BIBLE §9b). You're playing a cadre organization that *knows things through reports* — the Cadre Council fog design, the event-sourced intel ledger, the Veil of Money disclosure-by-theory mechanic all map naturally onto an interface where knowledge arrives as text and the map is what your organization has pieced together, not an omniscient satellite view. A terminal makes the epistemic fog feel like a property of the world instead of a UI gimmick.

On the technical side, the architecture already did the hard work. State is pure data, the engine is pure transformation, AI narrates but never adjudicates — so any frontend is just another observer-and-commander on the same seam the cockpit uses. Python-native **Textual** is the obvious fit: full mouse support (click, hover, scroll — your requirement is met out of the box), rich styling that can carry the ksbc palette, partial-refresh rendering that handles a few thousand counties without breaking a sweat, and `textual-web` can later serve the identical TUI in a browser, so it hedges rather than abandons the web investment.

The genuinely hard part is topology rendering, and it needs TUI-native idioms rather than a ported force-directed graph:

- **Spatial substrate**: a choropleth via half-block or braille characters works well. The Lawverian scale ladder (county < CZ < state < national) gives you *discrete* zoom levels — drill-down through the adjunction instead of continuous pan/zoom, which is both easier in a terminal and more honest about the model.
- **Relational topology** (solidarity/exploitation/tribute edges): force-directed layouts are poor in cells. Ego-network views (select a node, see its edges as an annotated tree), adjacency/intensity matrices, and ranked contradiction lists are the right vocabulary — and they match how the Interrogable Field design already thinks.

One constitutional note on "manipulate it": the substrate is immutable and the engine adjudicates, so the player never literally drags edges — they select and issue verbs (organizational actions through OODA). A TUI's selection-plus-command interaction model *enforces* that discipline naturally, where drag-and-drop UIs constantly tempt you to violate it.

The real costs are organizational, not technical: it's a second frontend seam to maintain alongside the cockpit (and the engine-bridge bug history shows seams are where defects breed), and it's a genuine program of work that would compete with the current spine. My honest recommendation: it deserves a brainstorm-to-spec cycle after the Vol III program lands, framed as either a cockpit sibling or successor — but as a feasibility question, the answer is yes, comfortably, and with unusual thematic payoff.

This clicks into place almost suspiciously well — to the point where I'd argue the wiki isn't a UI choice layered on Babylon, it's the diegetic form the game was already converging on. Let me give you the honest assessment, including where the framing needs one correction.

**Why it's structurally right.** A wiki is pages + links; BabylonGraph is nodes + typed edges. There's a nearly direct mapping: every entity (social class, territory, organization, key figure, contradiction) is a page; edges are links; backlinks are incoming edges ("what exploits this county"); categories are node types; transclusion is live stat blocks. It also solves the hardest TUI problem I flagged earlier — you can't meaningfully pan a 3,000-county force-directed graph in a terminal, but you don't have to: wiki navigation replaces the zoomable canvas with link-following, ego-network by ego-network. Page-embedded topology visualizations (choropleth blocks, ego-trees, opposition tension bars) do the local rendering where it's actually legible.

**Why it's diegetically right.** The third pillar of the Embedded Trinity is literally named The Archive. A revolutionary organization runs on documents — dossiers, reports, internal bulletins, party archives. The wiki *is the Cadre Council's own files*, which makes the fog design land for free: red links are entities you know exist but haven't investigated (the fog frontier as a wiki-native idiom), stale pages carry "last verified tick 34" stamps, the event-sourced intel ledger gives you page revision histories, and contradictory reports on a page are honest epistemic state, not a bug. Your own documentation constitution becomes an in-game law: the Archive never asserts what the ledger can't back — unverified claims are marked as rumor. That's the Verifiability principle turned into a game mechanic.

**The one correction to the framing.** Babylon doesn't *become* a templating engine — the wiki is a **materialized view** of graph state (your own Charity Majors quote applies: disposable render, durable state). This distinction is load-bearing constitutionally. Pages must be two-layered: deterministic template slots filled from ledger/graph state (the facts), with LLM narrative as an optional prose pass grounded in the tick's events — because AI parses/narrates only, the engine adjudicates, and nothing the LLM writes may enter the tick hash (the fog-epistemic ruling already establishes player knowledge stays out of the hash). The page must be fully informative with the narrator flagged off — the Program 20 narrator stack already established that flag-off discipline. Templates themselves should be data files per the Paradox Pattern, which incidentally makes the whole surface moddable.

**Where it gets genuinely novel: the write path.** The player never edits reality — but they *author documents*: directives, campaign plans, agitprop. Player-written pages are the command surface. You draft a directive from a template, the AI parses it into structured verbs (constitutionally sanctioned — "AI parses"), shows you the deterministic interpretation for confirmation, and the engine adjudicates. The game loop becomes: read the Archive → form a theory → write a directive → the engine answers → events revise the Archive. Reading and writing pages *is* the OODA loop. No other strategy game works like that.

**Risks worth naming now:** template sprawl (keep the template set small and data-driven); LLM latency/cost (render narrative lazily on page visit, cache by entity + tick, invalidate from events); and the classic second-source-of-truth trap — the wiki must always be regenerable from state, never authoritative itself. Textual handles the mechanics (Markdown widgets, link navigation, vim bindings, mouse) comfortably.

My verdict: this is the strongest frontend concept Babylon has had, stronger than the cockpit-as-dashboard framing, because it makes the interface itself materialist — knowledge as a produced, dated, revisable artifact. It deserves a full brainstorm-to-spec cycle after Vol III lands rather than a bolt-on.
