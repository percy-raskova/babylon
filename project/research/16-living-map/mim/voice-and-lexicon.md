# MIM Archive Voice & Lexicon Research

**Source:** `/media/user/data/mim/textonly/` (read-only archive, 9,109 files — the former
website of the Maoist Internationalist Movement / Maoist Internationalist Party-Amerika,
now mirrored by MIM(Prisons); also `/media/user/data/mim/mt10.pdf`, cross-read via its
HTML twin `mt/mt10intro.html`). Sampled ~55 documents stratified across `agitation/`,
`MIM.essays/`, `faq/`, `classics/`, `bpp/`, `countries/`, `gender/`, `wim/cong/` (party
congress documents), `mn/` and `nr/` (MIM Notes / Notas Rojas newspapers), `mt/` (MIM
Theory journal), `ulk/` (Under Lock & Key, prison newsletter), plus site-voice index pages
(`index.html`, `contents.html`, `faq/index.html`, `agitation/index.html`, `art/index.html`).

**Purpose:** extract the VOICE for the Babylon "Cockpit" UI — tone, vocabulary, framing —
so the interface reads as agitprop-literate, not a corporate SaaS dashboard, while staying
usable. This is a **voice/lexicon donor**, not an ideology the game endorses uncritically —
see Pitfalls at the end for where MIM's actual politics (some of it ugly: essentialist
"gender aristocracy" theory, glorification of Gonzalo/Sendero Luminoso) must NOT be echoed.

---

## 1. Voice: tone, register, rhythm

MIM's writing is **built to persuade an already-sympathetic-but-uncommitted reader through
moral urgency and taxonomic precision at the same time.** Two registers coexist in nearly
every document:

- **Newspaper register (MIM Notes / Notas Rojas / Under Lock & Key):** short declarative
  sentences, present-tense immediacy, ALL-CAPS or title-case shouting headlines, named
  actors and dates in the first two sentences (this is a literal house rule — see §4), scare
  quotes around euphemisms the state uses about itself.
  - `faq/mnstyle.html` (§2, "Political structure of articles"): *"Each article for MIM Notes
    starts with a news item in the first paragraph, preferably indicating main actors and
    dates by the end of the second sentence... If your article has no precise dates and
    names in it... don't expect it to go in MIM Notes."*
  - Headline sample, `ulk/106.html`: **"MARCH AGAINST PRISONS: MASSES REJECT BRUTALIZING
    'CORRECTIONS'"** — note the scare-quoted euphemism ("corrections") sitting right next
    to the plain word ("brutalizing").
  - Headline sample, `MIM.essays/500.years`: **"500 Years of Resistance to Colonization and
    Imperialism"** — dates as scale, not decoration.

- **Theory register (MIM Theory, FAQ, Congress documents):** longer periodic sentences,
  numbered enumerations of "characteristics," heavy citation apparatus (numbered endnotes,
  page numbers, publisher/city/year), a didactic Q→A structure. Confident, textbook-flat
  declaratives — theory is presented as settled science, not opinion.
  - `faq/imperialism.html`: *"Imperialism is the highest stage of capitalism according to
    those of us following Lenin..."* — states doctrine, then immediately gives concrete named
    examples (countries, named individuals: "Bush, Cheney, Rockefeller, Bill Gates...").
  - `wim/cong/fascismdef.html` numbers its defining characteristics 1–10, each a quoted
    citation from Dimitrov or Dutt — taxonomy as argument.

- **Anger is explicit policy, not accident.** `faq/tone.html` is a full essay arguing FOR
  the harsh register: *"a neutral tone is not appropriate when your friend is about to fall
  off a cliff. You better yell in excitement: 'Look out!'... we communists seek to match our
  tone to underlying substance."* This is directly useful: the game's "wire" feed should
  modulate tone with the state of the world, not stay evenly cheerful/neutral — a famine
  event should not read like a stock ticker.

- **Naming friend and enemy is a structural habit, not flavor.** Every institution gets
  sorted: oppressor nation / oppressed nation, imperialist / comprador, vanguard / labor
  bureaucracy, revolutionary / revisionist, principled criticism / opportunism. Nothing is
  presented as neutrally "complicated" — MIM's style is to render the material relation
  legible fast, then let the reader feel the weight of it.

### 10+ sample phrases worth echoing (rhythm/structure, not verbatim)

1. "The principal contradiction is between the oppressed nations and imperialism."
   (`faq/mnstyle.html`) — the "principal contradiction" framing is exactly Babylon's own
   dialectic primitive `D = (A, Ā, w, T, σ)`.
2. "Not an *ordinary succession* of one bourgeois government by another, but a
   *substitution*... by another form—open terrorist dictatorship." (`wim/cong/fascismdef.html`)
   — quantity-to-quality language, useful for an endgame-transition screen.
2b. "A neutral tone is not appropriate for the emergency situation that exists today."
   (`faq/tone.html`)
3. "We communists seek to match our tone to underlying substance." (`faq/tone.html` /
   `faq/amerika.html`, verbatim in both — clearly a repeated house line)
4. "Build public opinion and the independent institutions of the oppressed to seize power."
   (`faq/mnstyle.html`) — MIM's stated "principal task," good raw material for a mobilize-verb
   tooltip.
5. "The essence of all 'rights' is the power to enforce them." (`MIM.essays/500.years`)
6. "MIM believes it is a very good thing for diverse groups to unite in exposure... But to
   set no higher goal than co-existence with imperialism will perpetuate imperialism for
   another 500 years." (`MIM.essays/500.years`) — pattern: concede tactical unity, insist on
   strategic horizon.
7. "The backbone of the Maoist movement... has always been [the base]." (`MIM.essays/500.years`,
   re: Andes peasantry) — reusable structural sentence for describing a class base.
8. "Fascism strives to establish political and organizational unity among *all the governing
   classes*... their undivided, open and consistent dictatorship." (`wim/cong/fascismdef.html`)
9. "We depend on volunteers to get MIM Notes out... take it upon yourself." (`agitation/index.html`)
   — direct, second-person, no corporate hedging ("please consider").
10. "None of the participants thought that being at the rally would make a sudden change...
    But by [doing X]... we showed the state that we are watching." (`ulk/106.html`) — honest
    about limited tactical wins while framing them as part of a long war of position. Good
    model for a "minor victory" event toast that doesn't oversell itself.
11. "MIM is an underground party that does not publish the names of its comrades in order to
    avoid state surveillance and repression." (`mn/index.html`) — masthead register: terse,
    matter-of-fact about danger.
12. "The reduction of politics to mass adulation for a fetish is an insult to both politically
    backwards and advanced people." (`MIM.essays/critique.of.rcp`) — MIM's anti-personality-cult
    line; useful voice for critiquing charismatic-leader mechanics in-game without moralizing.

## 3. Visual culture (from the archive's design era)

The archive is 1990s–2000s static HTML: `<FONT COLOR="red">` banners, `<table>`-based
layout, small chunky GIF icons, red horizontal rules (`<hr color=red>`), a fixed three-icon
masthead repeated on nearly every page (`hc.gif` — hammer & sickle, `mim_head_long.gif` —
wordmark, `fists.gif` — raised fist), and content-specific icon buttons named after their
subject (`amlock.gif` for prisons, `ustank.gif` for militarism/9-11/the draft, `border.gif`
for Operation Gatekeeper, `broken-chains.gif` credited "art by a prisoner" on the Revolutionary
Art index). Category navigation is a literal icon grid (`agitation/index.html`), not a nav
bar — each cause gets one small woodcut-style badge.

Signal for Babylon's chrome:
- **Text-forward, image-sparse.** The archive trusts words over illustration; icons are
  labels, not hero art. Babylon's HUD should let dense text/numbers (the "every number
  explains itself" nested-inspection goal) carry weight rather than leaning on decorative
  illustration.
- **Red as the single accent, used structurally** (rules, warning banners, the masthead
  fist) not decoratively — consistent with the ratified Cold Collapse palette's cyan-accent
  discipline: one accent color doing real signaling work (alerts/status), not mood lighting.
- **Un-slick, deliberately.** Prisoner-drawn art credited by name/anonymity, hand-cut icon
  style — the aesthetic of a movement newsletter, not a media company. This licenses the
  Cockpit avoiding glossy stock-photo realism in event art in favor of woodcut/silkscreen/
  agit-poster-style iconography for wire events, IF the project wants illustrated events at
  all (current mandate is text-forward, so this is a "if ever" note).
- **Masthead repetition as identity anchor.** Every single page repeats the same three-icon
  banner regardless of content — a cheap, consistent "you are still inside MIM" signal. Analog
  for Babylon: a persistent, unchanging top-strip identity element (palette + wordmark +
  status glyph) that survives across every lens/panel so the player never loses the "this is
  Babylon" anchor while diving through nested inspection panels.

## 4. UI copy rules (write ALL in-game copy this way)

1. **Lead with actor + action + date/tick, not abstraction.** MIM's own house rule
   (`faq/mnstyle.html`): name who did what and when, in the first sentence. A wire headline
   should read "Detroit Auto Workers' Council seizes three plants, tick 214" not "Labor unrest
   intensifies in the Midwest."
2. **Name the contradiction, don't gesture at "complexity."** Every MIM document sorts actors
   into a relation (oppressor/oppressed, vanguard/bureaucracy). UI copy should say what is in
   tension with what, using Babylon's own primitive `D = (A, Ā, w, T, σ)` — never "things are
   complicated here."
3. **Modulate tone to the stakes, deliberately** (`faq/tone.html`). A famine event and a minor
   diplomatic snub should not read in the same even register. Reserve the sharpest language
   (imperative, urgent) for genuinely high-stakes ticks; a flat/urgent tone on every event
   trains players to ignore all of them.
4. **Prefer the concrete noun to the euphemism, and mark euphemisms when quoting the
   in-fiction state's own language.** MIM's scare-quote habit around official terms
   ("corrections," "development") is a legitimate UI move for showing an in-fiction
   government's propaganda language distinct from the sim's own narration — quote it, don't
   adopt it as the default register.
5. **Numbers are argument, not decoration.** MIM cites page numbers, dollar amounts, dates to
   the day. Every stat surfaced in the Cockpit should be traceable one click deeper (nested
   inspection panels) — this is both a MIM stylistic value and Babylon's own explicit design
   goal ("every number explains itself").
6. **Second person, direct address, no hedging, for player-facing instructions.** "Take it
   upon yourself" (`agitation/index.html`), not "you may wish to consider." Button labels and
   tooltips should be imperative and confident, matching MIM's confident declarative register
   — but see Pitfall #2 below on where this backfires.
7. **Short sentences for status/urgent copy; longer periodic sentences reserved for the
   theory/explainer layer.** Match MIM's own register split (newspaper vs. theory-journal) —
   wire headlines and HUD alerts stay terse; the nested "explain this number" drill-down
   panels can run longer, citation-style prose.
8. **Never present an endgame or major outcome as neutral.** MIM never narrates an event as
   if it had no stake in the outcome. A REVOLUTIONARY_VICTORY or FASCIST_CONSOLIDATION screen
   should read as consequential, not as a scoreboard.
9. **Consistent naming discipline beats cleverness.** MIM's own style guide fixes exact
   spellings/capitalizations site-wide (Black Nation not "African American," undocumented not
   "illegal," u.s. lowercase-with-periods) — Babylon should keep one small, written, enforced
   glossary of exactly how every recurring class/faction/territory name is spelled and
   capitalized, and apply it identically everywhere (wire, tooltips, panel titles).
10. **Credit the source of a number or claim inline where it matters to trust** — MIM's
    footnote habit exists because it is arguing with skeptics. In Babylon this becomes:
    tooltip explanations that show the formula/inputs behind a stat, not just the stat.

## 5. Pitfalls — where this voice would hurt usability or read as kitsch

1. **Do not literally misspell nation/faction names for shock value** (no "$" or "kkk"
   typography stunts). MIM's `Amerika`/`u.$.` spelling works in a 1990s print newsletter
   addressed to a self-selecting radical readership; in a game HUD read thousands of times
   per session it will read as costume, not conviction, and will actively hurt scanability
   (a "$" in running text breaks number-parsing at a glance, which directly conflicts with
   Babylon's "every number explains itself" numeracy goal). Get the *effect* (names encode
   material relations) through mechanics — territories are literally renamed on liberation —
   not through orthography.
2. **Second-person imperative copy ("Take it upon yourself," "Don't vote, Organize!") works
   for a militant's newsletter addressed to converts; it can read as hectoring when aimed at
   a player who is still learning the UI.** Reserve peak-imperative register for moments the
   player has opted into (declaring an action, confirming an endgame choice) — not for
   onboarding, empty states, or error messages, which need calm clarity instead.
3. **Do not import MIM's specific factional invective** (labeling real-world tendencies
   "revisionist," "opportunist," "social fascist," etc., as MIM does toward the RCP, CPUSA,
   Trotskyists). The *taxonomic instinct* (sort every actor by its relation to the principal
   contradiction) is reusable game-mechanically; the *specific historical grudges* are not
   Babylon's business and would read as an out-of-place real-world political statement rather
   than diegetic flavor.
4. **Do not import the "gender aristocracy" framework** (see Lexicon table) — it is MIM's
   weakest, most essentialist theoretical stretch and reads as dated/alienating even to
   sympathetic modern readers; it is also the single easiest place a reviewer would flag the
   game as endorsing something ugly rather than merely borrowing period voice.
5. **The archive glorifies specific, real, still-living political violence** (Gonzalo/Sendero
   Luminoso hagiography in `countries/peru/`: "the greatest communist leader in our
   hemisphere"). Babylon's in-fiction endgames (REVOLUTIONARY_VICTORY etc.) should draw on
   the *structural* vocabulary (people's war, mass line, united front) without importing
   real-world figure-worship — the sim's own generated leaders/events are the correct target
   for that narrative energy, not real historical actors MIM canonized.
6. **Density is a feature in a newsletter, a bug in a HUD.** MIM's theory register
   (footnoted, numbered, page-cited) is excellent for the *deep* nested-inspection layer but
   would be unreadable as a default HUD state — reserve it for the drill-down, not the
   surface.
7. **All-caps headline shouting reads as SPAM in a modern UI font stack**, even though it
   reads as urgent in a 1990s monospace newsletter. Get the same emphasis with weight/size/
   color in the existing Cold Collapse palette instead of literal caps-lock.
8. **MIM's tone essay itself warns against tone-for-its-own-sake** ("formalists" is their own
   pejorative for people "obsessed with tone, style or art for their own sake") — a useful
   internal check: if a piece of UI copy is doing a voice bit without also communicating a
   real state change, cut it. Voice must always ride on top of an actual number or event, per
   Rule 5 above, never substitute for one.
